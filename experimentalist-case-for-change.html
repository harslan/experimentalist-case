We Stand at a Crossroads

Every scholar should recognize this moment: the intersection of theory and evidence.

What Theory Suggests

Compelling reasons to believe 47 credits with choose-one model will serve students better:

  • Competitive positioning research
  • Advisory board feedback
  • Student agency literature
  • Retention research

What We Need

But theory alone isn't how we operate, is it?

Rigorous evidence from
controlled experimentation

The question isn't whether to change. It's whether we're scholars willing to test our assumptions.

The Core Proposition

This Is a Natural Experiment

This isn't a permanent, irreversible restructuring.
This is the kind of study we teach our students to value.

Publishable

Generates rigorous findings for peer-reviewed journals on business education

Fundable

Positions Suffolk for federal grants, foundation funding, AACSB recognition

Reversible

If results disappoint, we revert with data-informed confidence

Either Way, We Win

✓
If We're Right
  • Students benefit immediately from increased flexibility
  • We generate evidence of what works in business ed
  • Suffolk becomes a thought leader in curriculum innovation
  • Grants, publications, and AACSB recognition follow
↺
If We're Wrong
  • We learn something valuable about curriculum design
  • We document why traditional models matter
  • We publish that finding—negative results are knowledge
  • We revert with data-informed confidence

Both outcomes advance knowledge. Only blocking the experiment advances nothing.

Strategic Opportunity

The Funding Landscape Favors Innovators

Evidence-based curriculum reform is exactly what federal funders want to see. The Department of Education, NSF, and foundations are hungry for rigorous studies of what actually works.

Dept. of Education

FIPSE grants prioritize scalable innovations with measurable outcomes

NSF

IUSE grants fund curriculum experiments with rigorous assessment designs

Foundations

Lumina, Gates, and others seek student success innovations

This change positions us to pursue funding that would be impossible if we simply maintained the status quo without proof.

To Colleagues Who Hesitate

I understand the instinct to protect what works. But consider what blocking this experiment means:

Blocking the Experiment

  • Protecting assumptions over testing them
  • Choosing intuition over inquiry
  • Opposite of what we ask students to do
  • Foreclosing grant opportunities

Engaging as a Scholar

  • Bring critical eye to evaluation design
  • Help define metrics that matter
  • Challenge assumptions within study
  • Become a co-author on findings

Don't be a barrier to knowledge creation. Be a co-investigator.
